How to Identify 5 Common Panfish in Canada & the US

Bluegill, pumpkinseed, crappie, yellow perch, and warmouth — learn to tell them apart at a glance.

Panfish are the most accessible freshwater fish in North America. They're the fish many of us caught as kids, and they remain a favourite for anglers of all skill levels. But with so many similar-looking species, telling them apart can be tricky. Here's a simple guide to the five most common panfish across Canada and the United States, with key identification tips for each.

1. Bluegill (Lepomis macrochirus)

Range: Found across southern Canada and throughout the continental US

Key identifiers:

  • Dark blue or black ear flap (operculum flap) — solid colour, no edge markings
  • Vertical dark bars on the sides, typically 5-9 faint bars
  • Small mouth that doesn't extend past the eye
  • Colour: Olive-green back, yellow-orange belly, bluish highlights on the face
  • Size: Typically 15-25 cm (6-10 in), up to 2 kg (4 lb)

Bluegill are the most widespread panfish in North America. They prefer warm, vegetated lakes and slow-moving rivers. They school in large numbers, making them excellent for introducing kids to fishing. They're aggressive biters and will hit worms, small jigs, and even dry flies.

2. Pumpkinseed (Lepomis gibbosus)

Range: Southern Canada and northeastern US, introduced in some western states

Key identifiers:

  • Short, dark ear flap with a bright red or orange spot on the edge
  • Wavy blue lines on the face and gill cover — like a fingerprint
  • Orange or yellow belly with a speckled, golden-brown body
  • Body shape: Deep, compressed, and almost round — like a pumpkin seed
  • Size: Typically 15-20 cm (6-8 in), rarely over 0.5 kg (1 lb)

Pumpkinseed are among the most colourful freshwater fish in North America. Their bright colours make them a favourite for photographers and nature enthusiasts. They're found in weedy shallows and are common in cottage-country lakes across Ontario, Quebec, and the northeastern states.

3. Crappie (Black & White) (Pomoxis nigromaculatus & Pomoxis annularis)

Range: Across most of the US and southern Canada

Key identifiers:

  • Large, thin body — crappie are much more elongated than sunfish
  • Large mouth that extends past the eye (unlike bluegill)
  • Dorsal fin with 7-8 spines (sunfish have 10-12)
  • Black crappie: Irregular dark blotches all over the body and fins
  • White crappie: 5-10 vertical dark bars on a silvery body
  • Size: Typically 20-30 cm (8-12 in), up to 2 kg (4 lb)

Crappie are highly sought-after panfish that school in large numbers. Black crappie prefer clearer, vegetated waters while white crappie tolerate murkier conditions. They're both excellent eating fish. Spring spawning season (April-June) is the best time to target them as they move into shallow bays and marinas.

4. Yellow Perch (Perca flavescens)

Range: Across Canada and the northern United States

Key identifiers:

  • Yellow-gold body with 5-8 bold dark vertical bars
  • Two dorsal fins — the first is spiny, the second is soft-rayed (sunfish have a single continuous dorsal fin)
  • Orange lower fins (pectoral, pelvic, and anal fins)
  • Body shape: More elongated than sunfish, with a slight humpback
  • Size: Typically 15-25 cm (6-10 in), up to 1 kg (2 lb)

Yellow perch are unrelated to sunfish — they belong to the perch family (Percidae). They're one of the most popular panfish for eating, with firm white meat. Perch travel in large schools and can be caught through the ice in winter. They prefer cool, clear lakes with sandy or gravel bottoms.

5. Warmouth (Lepomis gulosus)

Range: Southeastern Canada (Lake Erie region) and eastern US, particularly the south

Key identifiers:

  • Large mouth that extends past the eye — the largest mouth of any sunfish
  • Dark brown or olive body with mottled, irregular markings
  • Red or orange eye — a distinctive feature
  • Short, rounded ear flap with a pale or white edge
  • Size: Typically 15-20 cm (6-8 in), up to 1 kg (2 lb)

Warmouth are often mistaken for rock bass or green sunfish. They're more predatory than other sunfish and will hit larger lures. They prefer warm, slow-moving, vegetated waters and are often found in the same areas as largemouth bass. In Canada, they're primarily found in southern Ontario near Lake Erie and Lake Ontario.

Tips for Identifying Panfish

  • Look at the ear flap — The coloured flap on the gill cover is the easiest way to tell sunfish species apart.
  • Count the dorsal spines — Sunfish have 10-12, crappie have 7-8, and perch have two separate fins.
  • Check the mouth — If it extends past the eye, it's either a crappie or warmouth, not a bluegill or pumpkinseed.
  • Note the body shape — Deep and round means sunfish; thin and tall means crappie; striped and elongated means perch.
